The Effect of Different Wavelengths of Light on Ampicillin Susceptibility in Escherichia coli

Sophmore Research Project

Background

Visible and UV light stress bacteria populations significantly. Research indicates that light with wavelengths between 250 and 488 nm leads to a substantial decrease in the viable cell density of E. coli, often by 7.85 orders of magnitude. UV resistance in E. coli is linked to resistance against other environmental stresses. UV-selected populations displayed approximately twofold increased resistance to desiccation and starvation but were tenfold more vulnerable to hypersalinity. These findings suggest that UV resistance variations in bacterial populations persist due to their impact on resistance against other environmental factors. Additionally, mutations that enhance E. coli fitness in antibiotic-free growth media can also boost antibiotic resistance. Notably, 3.5% of the bacteria populations exhibited reduced susceptibility, while 0.6% displayed increased susceptibility, underscoring how diverse growth conditions foster genetic variation for resistance mutations that natural selection can act upon following antibiotic exposure.

Rational

Antibiotic resistance poses a mounting global health threat, with the US alone recording over 2.8 million infections and 35,000 deaths. These infections curtail treatment choices, result in higher medical expenses, longer hospitalizations, and elevated mortality rates. To combat this crisis effectively, it's crucial to understand how environmental elements, such as exposure to various light wavelengths, play a role in antibiotic resistance development. The findings of the study may inform the utilization of UV light as a disinfectant to decrease antibiotic-resistant populations.

Experimental Design

The objective of the project is to investigate how the evolution of UV resistance affects antibiotic susceptibility. The independent variable is the wavelength of light that the E. coli population was exposed to. The experimental groups are the no light (control), UV light (390 - 400 nm), and visible light (400 - 700 nm) groups. The dependent variable is the diameter of the zone of inhibition (the circle around a disk with no bacterial growth) in centimeters. A larger zone of inhibition indicates that the bacteria is more susceptible to the antibiotic, while a smaller zone indicates that the bacteria is less susceptible. The hypothesis was that one reason that ampicillin susceptibility in E. coli varies is that environmental factors like exposure to UV light increase the genetic variation of antibiotic-resistant genes that natural selection can act on, decreasing ampicillin susceptibility. It was predicted that the exposure to UV light would result in a significantly smaller diameter zone of inhibition in an E. coli population, while exposure to visible light would result in no significant difference in the diameter of the zone of inhibition.
